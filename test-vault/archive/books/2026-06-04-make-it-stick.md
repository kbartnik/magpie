# Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning — Brown, Roediger, McDaniel (2014)

**Source:** `make-it-stick.pdf`
**Coverage:** Retrieval practice, spaced repetition, interleaving, desirable difficulties, illusions of knowing, massed practice failure, applying learning science

## The Central Argument

The study strategies that feel most effective are the least effective for durable learning. Re-reading and massed practice (cramming) produce **fluency** — the feeling of knowing — but this feeling is unreliable. The strategies that feel hardest and produce the most errors during practice are the ones that produce the most durable, transferable learning.

## Retrieval Practice (The Testing Effect)

Testing yourself on material is more effective for long-term retention than re-reading the same material. The act of *retrieving* a memory strengthens it in ways that re-exposure does not.

**Why it works:** Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive. Each retrieval re-encodes the memory, integrating it with current knowledge and strengthening the retrieval pathway. The effort of retrieval is the mechanism of learning — not the recognition of familiarity.

**Practical form:** Flashcards, practice problems, self-quizzing, the Feynman technique (explain the concept without notes). The form matters less than the retrieval act.

## Spaced Repetition

Reviewing material at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks...) produces far more durable retention than massed review. The spacing effect was discovered by Ebbinghaus in 1885 and is among the most replicated findings in cognitive psychology.

**Why it works:** Forgetting is not the enemy of learning — some forgetting is necessary for spacing to work. The partial forgetting between review sessions forces harder retrieval, which strengthens the memory more. Review before complete forgetting ensures the memory survives; the gap ensures the retrieval is effortful.

**Practical form:** Spaced repetition software (Anki, SuperMemo) implements optimal spacing algorithmically using the SM-2 algorithm or variants.

## Interleaving

Practicing different types of problems or topics in mixed order (ABCABC) rather than blocked order (AABBCC) improves the ability to discriminate between types and choose the right approach.

**Why it works:** Blocked practice optimizes performance during practice; interleaved practice optimizes performance on transfer. Interleaving forces the learner to actively identify which approach applies — the discrimination skill that transfers to real-world problems.

**Desirable difficulty:** The term the authors use for these strategies. They're desirable because the difficulty is productive — it generates learning. Undesirable difficulty (confusing instructions, illegible text) generates frustration without learning.

## Illusions of Knowing

People systematically overestimate their mastery. Fluency during re-reading feels like knowledge but is recognition, not recall. The feeling of knowing is not correlated with the ability to retrieve.

**Calibration:** The ability to accurately assess one's own competence is a metacognitive skill (see Metacognition). Poor calibration leads to stopping practice too early ("I know this") and starting tests underprepared.

## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Learning requires a processing chain: *encode → retrieve → reconsolidate*. Passive exposure (reading, listening) hits only the first step. Retrieval practice completes the chain. The research is unambiguous and the practical implication is simple: replace re-reading time with retrieval practice time.

**What Surprised Me:** The illusion of knowing is not a sign of intellectual weakness — it's a normal feature of memory. Familiarity (recognition) and recall are different memory systems, and familiarity is faster and more available. The brain defaults to "I've seen this before" as a proxy for "I know this." Breaking this illusion requires external calibration: tests, flashcards, teaching — anything that forces recall rather than recognition.

**Open Questions:**
- Spaced repetition software optimizes spacing based on self-reported difficulty. How sensitive is the spacing algorithm to calibration errors? If a learner consistently rates cards as easier than they are, how quickly does the algorithm degrade?
- Interleaving improves transfer but reduces performance during practice. In domains with fast feedback cycles (programming, music), does this tradeoff resolve faster or slower than in academic domains?
- Retrieval practice strengthens memory traces but can also strengthen incorrect memories. Is there evidence that self-testing with incorrect answers (before knowing the right answer) causes more harm than benefit?
