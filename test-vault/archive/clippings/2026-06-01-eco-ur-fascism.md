Essay delivered as a speech at Columbia University in 1995, then published in NYRB on June 22, 1995. Eco reflects on his childhood under Italian fascism and proposes a framework for recognizing fascism across its many historical forms — not as a fixed ideology but as a family of features, any one of which can nucleate a fascist movement.

## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Fascism has no quintessence — it is "fuzzy totalitarianism," a structured confusion held together by emotional/archetypal foundations rather than philosophical coherence. The implication is diagnostic: you cannot debunk fascism by exposing its contradictions, because contradiction is constitutive, not a weakness.

**What Surprised Me:** In 1995, Eco explicitly predicted "TV or Internet populism, in which the emotional response of a selected group of citizens can be presented and accepted as the Voice of the People" (feature 13). He named the mechanism of social media demagogy thirty years before it became a dominant political reality.

**Open Questions:**
- Feature 6 (fascism exploits a frustrated middle class) assumes the lumpen/underclass is "largely excluded from the political scene." Does this hold in contemporary populist movements, or has the Internet changed recruitment patterns?
- Eco distinguishes Ur-Fascism from classical totalitarianism by its *philosophical incoherence*. Does this distinction hold for 21st-century authoritarian movements, which often combine systematic legal strategy with emotional chaos?
- If any single feature is sufficient to "allow fascism to coagulate around it," is the framework at risk of over-classification? What distinguishes a fascist movement from a merely authoritarian or nationalist one?
