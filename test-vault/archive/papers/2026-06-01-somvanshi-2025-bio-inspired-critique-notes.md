## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Over one-third of published bio-inspired solvers are reformulations or simplifications of classical algorithms wrapped in metaphor-driven terminology — "novelty inflation" that dilutes scientific rigor without advancing the field. The paper argues the community needs formal novelty standards, not just peer review.

**What Surprised Me:** Somvanshi's 8-category taxonomy (evolutionary, swarm intelligence, physics-inspired, ecosystem/plant, predator-prey, neural-inspired, human-inspired, hybrid) independently matches the taxonomy already recorded in this vault's [[Bio-Inspired Computing]] page from Kar's earlier work — two separate sources converging on the same classification is strong evidence this is emerging field consensus.

**Open Questions:**
- What formal criteria would distinguish a legitimately novel bio-inspired algorithm from a metaphor-wrapped reformulation? The paper diagnoses the problem clearly but doesn't propose a reproducible novelty test.
- Open challenges listed (scalability, convergence, reliability, interpretability) are identical to what Del Ser 2019 identified 6 years earlier. This suggests either that these are genuinely hard problems or that the field is not making progress on its own stated priorities. Which is it?
- This is arXiv v4 (Sep 2025) — the high revision count suggests significant peer feedback. What changed between v1 and v4?
