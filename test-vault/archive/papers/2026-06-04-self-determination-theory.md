# Self-Determination Theory: Basic Psychological Needs in Motivation, Development, and Wellness

**Citation:** Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2000). The "what" and "why" of goal pursuits: Human needs and the self-determination of behavior. *Psychological Inquiry*, 11(4), 227–268.
**Coverage:** Intrinsic vs extrinsic motivation, the three basic psychological needs (competence, autonomy, relatedness), cognitive evaluation theory, organismic integration theory, self-determination continuum

## The Central Framework

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) proposes that humans have three **basic psychological needs** whose satisfaction supports intrinsic motivation, psychological growth, and well-being:

**Competence:** Feeling effective — the experience of mastering challenging tasks, producing desired outcomes. Competence need satisfaction is undermined by optimal challenges that are too easy (no growth signal) or too hard (repeated failure).

**Autonomy:** Feeling self-authored — experiencing one's behavior as volitional, not controlled. Autonomy is not independence; it's the sense that one is acting from personal values and interests rather than external pressure.

**Relatedness:** Feeling connected — experiencing warm, caring relationships. Relatedness need is less directly tied to learning outcomes but is foundational for trust in instructional relationships.

## Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

**Intrinsic motivation:** Engaging in an activity for its own inherent satisfaction — curiosity, challenge, aesthetic pleasure. Intrinsically motivated learning is self-sustaining, produces deeper processing, and transfers better.

**Extrinsic motivation:** Engaging for separable outcomes — grades, praise, payment, avoidance of punishment. SDT does not treat extrinsic motivation as uniformly bad, but distinguishes *types* by degree of internalization:

1. **External regulation:** "I do this to get the reward / avoid punishment." Fully controlled.
2. **Introjected regulation:** "I do this because I'd feel guilty otherwise." Internally pressured.
3. **Identified regulation:** "I do this because it's important to my goals." Partially self-determined.
4. **Integrated regulation:** "I do this because it reflects my values." Fully internalized — functionally similar to intrinsic motivation.

## Cognitive Evaluation Theory: How Rewards Undermine Intrinsic Motivation

When extrinsic rewards are applied to already-intrinsically-interesting activities, intrinsic motivation decreases — the **over-justification effect**. Rewards shift the perceived locus of causality from internal ("I do this because I find it interesting") to external ("I do this for the reward").

**Controlling language** (you *must*, you *should*, you *have to*) has the same effect even without material rewards. Informational feedback ("here's how you can improve") supports competence without undermining autonomy.

## Relevance for Learning and Education

SDT predicts that learning environments which support autonomy, provide optimal challenges, and offer informational rather than controlling feedback will produce higher intrinsic motivation, deeper processing, better retention, and greater transfer than controlling environments even if the controlling environment achieves higher short-term performance.

## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Motivation is not a fixed trait — it's produced by environmental conditions. The same person can be highly intrinsically motivated in a supportive environment and demotivated in a controlling one. This has a practical implication: designing the learning environment (or work environment) for autonomy, competence, and relatedness is not soft management — it's an intervention with measurable effects on performance and retention.

**What Surprised Me:** Informational praise ("you tried three different approaches before finding one that worked") supports competence and autonomy. Controlling praise ("you're so smart") undermines autonomy by making the learner feel evaluated rather than effective. The same surface-level positive feedback can support or undermine intrinsic motivation depending on whether it carries a controlling message.

**Open Questions:**
- ADHD involves specific impairments to the reward system (dopamine dysregulation) that affect motivation directly. Does SDT's competence/autonomy/relatedness framework apply similarly to ADHD individuals, or do the altered reward pathways change what environments support motivation?
- AI tutoring systems can offer highly personalized challenges (supporting competence) and choice of learning paths (supporting autonomy). But the interaction is still with a system, not a person. Can AI instruction satisfy the relatedness need, or is this a fundamental limitation?
- Internalization (moving from external to integrated regulation) takes time and requires social context. In fully self-directed online learning, where relatedness is weak, do learners tend to stay at lower internalization levels?
