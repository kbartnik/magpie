## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Tripp & Wickens propose the **dopamine transfer deficit** as a unifying account of ADHD: dopamine cells normally fire to rewarding events and then "transfer" their firing to earlier predictive cues as behavior becomes learned. ADHD may involve a failure of this transfer — the system remains hypersensitive to *immediate* reward and fails to predict delayed ones, explaining delay intolerance, impulsivity, and the characteristic ADHD response to immediate feedback.

**What Surprised Me:** The heritability of ADHD is ~0.8 (twin studies), comparable to height — yet no single gene accounts for more than ~3.6% of symptom variance. The genetic architecture is massively polygenic, which has direct implications for biomarker development: there's no "ADHD gene" to test for.

**Open Questions:**
- If the dopamine transfer deficit model is correct, do stimulants work by compensating for this deficit (enhancing prediction signals) rather than simply increasing dopamine availability?
- The heterogeneity of ADHD (504 ways to meet DSM criteria) suggests possible distinct subtypes — does the dopamine transfer deficit account for all subtypes, or mainly the hyperactive/impulsive presentation?
- What's the relationship between environmental risk factors (prenatal smoking, lead exposure, PCBs) and the dopaminergic mechanisms — do they act on the same pathways?
