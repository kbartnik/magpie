## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Bio-inspired computing is a philosophy, not a field — linking AI, evolutionary computation, biorobotics, and artificial life through the principle that robust, adaptive, emergent systems (arising from many loosely coupled processes) are more valuable in poorly-defined environments than fast, centralized ones.

**What Surprised Me:** Bongard frames Brooks' "Elephants Don't Play Chess" (embodied cognition — intelligence requires a body to interact with and learn from the environment) as the catalyst for AI's recovery from its 1990s winter. Wikipedia's emergence with minimal centralized editorial control is offered as a contemporary proof of concept for bio-inspired principles at scale.

**Open Questions:**
- In 2025, gradient descent + massive compute represents a maximally centralized AI approach that dominates benchmarks. Does this resolve the centralized vs. decentralized debate, or is the "robustness in novel environments" argument still live?
- Bongard uses Wikipedia as an emergence success story; LLMs trained on centralized pipelines now generate most knowledge summaries. Does this complicate or refute the emergence argument?
- AI winters were characterized by hype followed by funding collapse. Is current LLM investment a structural break from that pattern, or a new form of the same cycle?
