## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Barkley's hybrid model: ADHD is fundamentally a disorder of *behavioral inhibition*, not attention. Inattention and working memory deficits are downstream consequences of impaired inhibition — if you cannot inhibit prepotent responses, you cannot hold a mental representation in working memory long enough to act on it. This reframes ADHD: the problem isn't that attention is broken; it's that the *stop* signal is weaker, which then cascades into every downstream executive function.

**What Surprised Me:** Barkley distinguishes *retrospective* working memory (verbal: self-talk, rule-following, internalized speech) from *prospective* working memory (nonverbal: the ability to hold a mental image or plan and work toward it across time). ADHD specifically impairs prospective, nonverbal working memory — the ability to "see" a future state and keep it active long enough to guide behavior. This explains why ADHD individuals often understand rules and intentions but still fail to apply them in the moment: the prospective representation decays before it can influence action.

**Open Questions:**
- Barkley wrote this in 1997, before neuroimaging was routine. The behavioral inhibition model was inferential. How well have his predictions held up in the subsequent 25+ years of imaging studies? The frontostriatal circuit evidence largely supports him, but does the directionality (inhibition → WM, not WM → inhibition) hold up?
- The hybrid model treats the three EF components (inhibition, WM updating, flexibility) as hierarchical — inhibition is foundational. But newer EF research treats them as correlated but separable. Is the hierarchy real or a modeling assumption?
- If prospective nonverbal WM is the core deficit, stimulant medications work by improving dopamine signaling in the frontostriatal circuit — but do they specifically improve prospective WM, or do they improve inhibition first and WM secondarily?
