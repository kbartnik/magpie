# Metacognition and Self-Regulated Learning: A Review

**Citation:** Schraw, G., & McCrudden, M. (2006). Metacognition and self-regulated learning. *Educational Psychology Review*, 18, 111–140.
**Coverage:** Metacognitive knowledge, metacognitive regulation, calibration, monitoring, self-regulated learning cycle, development of metacognition, instructional implications

## What Metacognition Is

Metacognition is cognition about cognition — the ability to monitor and regulate one's own thinking. Flavell (1979) introduced the term; subsequent research has distinguished two components:

**Metacognitive knowledge:** What you know about how cognition works.
- *Person knowledge:* understanding your own cognitive strengths and weaknesses
- *Task knowledge:* understanding what a given task demands
- *Strategy knowledge:* knowing which strategies work for which tasks

**Metacognitive regulation:** Using metacognitive knowledge to control your learning.
- *Planning:* selecting strategies, allocating time and attention before beginning
- *Monitoring:* tracking comprehension and performance during a task
- *Evaluation:* assessing outcomes and adjusting strategy after a task

## The Calibration Problem

Calibration is the correspondence between a learner's confidence in their knowledge and their actual performance. Well-calibrated learners know what they know and don't know. Poorly-calibrated learners overestimate their knowledge (a failure of monitoring).

**The Dunning-Kruger effect** is a calibration failure: people with low competence in a domain are systematically overconfident, while people with high competence are systematically underconfident. The proposed mechanism: competence in a domain requires developing the same cognitive machinery needed to evaluate performance in that domain. Beginners lack the schema to evaluate what good performance looks like.

**Practical consequence:** Self-assessment is a poor substitute for external assessment in the early stages of skill acquisition. Tests, code review, teacher feedback — external signals — are necessary to bootstrap accurate metacognitive knowledge.

## Self-Regulated Learning

Self-regulated learners set learning goals, monitor their progress toward those goals, adjust strategies when progress stalls, and evaluate outcomes. This cycle — plan, monitor, evaluate, adjust — is what distinguishes effective from ineffective learners given identical study time.

Self-regulation is learned. Explicit instruction in metacognitive strategies (prompting students to self-explain, self-quiz, monitor comprehension) improves both metacognitive accuracy and learning outcomes. It is not a fixed trait.

## Development and Individual Differences

Metacognitive ability develops significantly through adolescence and into early adulthood — tracking the development of executive function and prefrontal cortex maturation. Children's monitoring is unreliable; adults with high metacognitive skill have better learning outcomes across domains.

ADHD disrupts metacognitive regulation specifically: the executive function deficits (impaired monitoring, difficulty sustaining strategic effort) map directly onto the regulatory component of metacognition.

## Deep Read

**Key Insight:** Metacognition is the executive control layer of learning. The knowledge (what I know, what the task demands, which strategies work) enables the regulation (plan, monitor, evaluate), and the regulation is what transforms effort into learning. Without metacognitive regulation, study time is spent in comfortable repetition rather than deliberate improvement.

**What Surprised Me:** Metacognitive accuracy is trainable through explicit instruction, but the instruction must be specific to the domain. Generic "think about your thinking" advice transfers poorly. What works: prompting self-explanation immediately after learning ("in your own words..."), calibration feedback ("you said 80% confident, you were right 60% of the time"), and worked examples of what good performance looks like.

**Open Questions:**
- Dunning-Kruger is one of the most replicated effects in social psychology but also one of the most contested statistically (some argue it's a mathematical artifact of the regression approach). Is it a real cognitive phenomenon or a methodological artifact?
- Self-regulated learning requires accurate task knowledge (knowing what the task demands). In novel domains, this knowledge doesn't exist yet. How do effective learners bootstrap task knowledge before they have the schema to evaluate their performance?
- AI tutoring systems (Khanmigo, etc.) can provide immediate calibration feedback that human teachers often can't at scale. Does AI-mediated metacognitive feedback produce learning outcomes comparable to teacher feedback, or does the social and contextual richness of human feedback matter?
