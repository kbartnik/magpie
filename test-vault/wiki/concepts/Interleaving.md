# Interleaving

Practicing different types of problems in mixed order (ABCABC) rather than blocked order (AABBCC) improves the ability to discriminate between problem types and choose the right approach.

## Why It Works

Blocked practice optimizes **performance during practice** by reducing the need to identify which approach applies — the type is given by the block.

Interleaved practice optimizes **performance on transfer** by forcing the learner to actively identify which approach applies for each problem. This discrimination skill is what transfers to real-world problems.

## The Performance Paradox

Interleaving performs *worse* during practice than blocked practice but *better* at test time. Learners and instructors often resist interleaving because it looks less effective in the short run. This is the desirable difficulty in action.

## Applications

- **Mathematics:** Mix problem types (derivatives, integrals, limits) rather than blocking by type
- **Programming:** Mix different algorithm types rather than drilling one pattern exhaustively
- **Music:** Practice different pieces or techniques in the same session rather than blocking by piece

- [[Desirable Difficulties]] — interleaving is a paradigmatic desirable difficulty; the short-term performance cost produces long-term transfer gain
