# Transformative Justice

An approach to harm, accountability, and conflict that centers healing, restoration of relationships, and community transformation — as an alternative to punishment-based responses (incarceration, policing, retributive justice).

Transformative justice (TJ) distinguishes itself from *restorative justice* by addressing not just the immediate harm but the underlying social conditions (poverty, racism, isolation) that enabled it. The goal is not to return to the status quo before the harm, but to build conditions that prevent recurrence.

## Core Principles

- Harm is real and must be addressed — TJ does not minimize or dismiss it
- Accountability is supported by community, not imposed by the state
- Survivors/victims center the response — what do *they* need?
- Transformation of conditions, not just punishment of individuals

## Relationship to [[Community Self-Defense]]

Activist community self-defense frameworks explicitly invoke transformative justice as the justice model that makes state/police responses illegitimate: if the state's justice system (police + prisons) reproduces the harms it claims to address, communities need alternative safety and accountability structures.

> "For abolitionists who prefer transformative justice to incarceration, police are not the answer to community safety." — *A Return to Leftist Self-Defense*, Yes! Magazine 2024
