# Mutual Aid

Voluntary, reciprocal exchange of resources and services within a community — without state mediation, charity-model hierarchy, or means-testing. Distinguished from charity by its horizontal structure: participants are both givers and recipients, not separated into providers and beneficiaries.

The concept has roots in anarchist and socialist theory (Kropotkin's *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution*, 1902) and has been continuously practiced by social movements as both a survival strategy and a political stance.

## Historical Examples in This Vault's Context

The [[Community Self-Defense]] lineage runs directly through mutual aid practice:
- **Black Panther Party** (1966+) — free breakfast programs, Black People's Free Store, health clinics; mutual aid as the material basis for community legitimacy and organizing power
- **Yellow Peril Tactical** (2020+) — "networks of care" alongside firearms training; mutual aid as visibility infrastructure
- **IWW General Defense Committee** — material support for members facing reprisals

## Mutual Aid vs. Charity

| Mutual Aid | Charity |
|-----------|---------|
| Horizontal, reciprocal | Hierarchical, one-directional |
| No eligibility gatekeeping | Means-testing and conditions |
| Community-led and defined | Institution or donor-defined |
| Builds collective power | Maintains dependency relationships |

## Connection to Community Safety

Mutual aid functions as both a material resource and a trust-building mechanism. Communities with strong mutual aid networks are harder to divide (less susceptibility to [[Strategic Racism]]) and have practical alternatives to state/police responses to harm ([[Transformative Justice]]).
