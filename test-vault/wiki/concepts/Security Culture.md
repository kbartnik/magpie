# Security Culture

A set of customs shared by a community whose members may be targeted by the government, designed to minimize risk through habitual practice rather than active vigilance.

## Core Principle

**Need-to-know only.** People should never be privy to sensitive information they do not need to know. Compartmentalization protects not just the project, but the people kept out — they cannot be coerced into revealing what they don't know.

## Why Habits Beat Vigilance

The distinction between *protocol* and *culture* is that culture becomes unconscious and effortless. Once safe behavior is habitual, participants spend less energy managing security and less time in paranoia — because they know they're already doing everything they can.

A key implication: **a good security culture makes infiltrators irrelevant.** If information never leaves the circle it concerns, an informant has nothing to report.

## The 7-Level Security Rating System

CrimethInc. proposes matching security level to action type:

| Level | Who Knows | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | Only direct participants | Firebombing a target |
| 2 | + trusted support persons (by group vote) | Minor property destruction |
| 3–4 | Those invited who might decline | Spokescouncil, newspaper wrap |
| 5 | "Rumors" spread, organizers anonymous | Surprise unpermitted march |
| 6 | Announced with some discretion | Critical Mass bicycle ride |
| 7 | Fully public | Permitted march, film screening |

Each level maps to a corresponding communications security level (in-person only → encrypted email → open channels).

## As Etiquette

Security culture is a form of social etiquette, not exclusion. The norms are:
- Don't ask others about sensitive matters you don't need to know
- Don't take it personally if others won't include you in something
- Say no to anything you're not comfortable with — including pressure from comrades
- Let others know your own security needs early in any collaboration

## The Paranoia Trap

Security culture is *anti-paranoia* infrastructure. Excessive suspicion of potential infiltrators wastes energy. If your culture is functioning correctly, it doesn't matter whether infiltrators are present — that's the test of a good culture.
