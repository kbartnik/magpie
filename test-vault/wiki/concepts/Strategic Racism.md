# Strategic Racism

The deliberate use of racial division by elites — wealthy individuals, corporations, and the politicians they fund — to prevent cross-racial working-class solidarity and protect concentrated economic and political power. Distinguished from organic or interpersonal racism by its instrumental, top-down character.

The mechanism: when working people of different racial backgrounds might otherwise unite around shared economic interests (wages, healthcare, housing), strategically deployed racial scapegoating redirects blame toward communities of color and fractures potential coalitions.

## Origins of the Concept

The term appears prominently in the [[Race Class Narrative]] framework developed by Ian Haney López, Heather McGhee, and Anat Shenker-Osorio. Their research found that directly *naming* strategic racism — explaining it as an elite tactic — is more persuasive across demographic groups than either avoiding racial discussion (color-blind messaging) or discussing racism in purely moral terms.

From McGhee's *The Sum of Us*:
> "color-blind approaches that ignored racism didn't beat the scapegoating zero-sum story; we had to be honest about racism's role in dividing us in order to call people to their higher ideals."

## Key Insight for Messaging

Strategic racism is powerful precisely because it's usually implicit. Effective counter-messaging names the tactic explicitly: "the 1% and the politicians they pay for are dividing us based on race" — then pivots to cross-racial shared interest. This is more effective than either ignoring the racial frame or responding only with moral arguments about equality.

## Relationship to Other Concepts

- **[[Race Class Narrative]]** — the messaging framework designed to counter strategic racism
- **[[Community Self-Defense]]** — strategic racism has historically provided ideological cover for physical violence against organizing left movements (KKK harassment of IWW, Proud Boys targeting labor pickets)
- **[[Mutual Aid]]** — mutual aid networks are one structural counter: when communities directly meet each other's needs across racial lines, strategic divisibility decreases
- **[[Ur-Fascism]]** — Eco's features 5–8 describe the same mechanism at the ideological level: fear of difference, exploitation of frustration, conspiracy obsession, and rhetorical oscillation between enemy-as-threat and enemy-as-weak. Strategic racism is the contemporary practice of what Eco analyzed as the emotional engine of fascism.
