# Deliberate Practice

Practice specifically designed to improve performance, at the limits of current competence, with immediate feedback. The quality of practice, not the quantity, is what separates experts from long-time practitioners.

## Four Characteristics

1. **Task at the limit of competence** — not so easy it requires no effort; not so hard it produces no useful signal. The zone of proximal development.
2. **Immediate, informative feedback** — you know whether each attempt succeeded and why. A teacher, a recording, a test suite.
3. **Full concentration** — deliberate practice cannot be maintained for long periods. Top performers sustain 2–4 hours per day before quality degrades.
4. **Repetition with refinement** — the same challenging component attempted repeatedly, each attempt informed by feedback from the last.

## The 10,000-Hour Misreading

*Outliers* (Gladwell) popularized "10,000 hours = expertise." This misrepresents Ericsson's finding. The actual claim:

- Expertise requires roughly **10 years** (not hours) of sustained deliberate practice
- The hours matter less than whether they were **deliberate**
- The 10,000-hour figure is a descriptive average, not a prescriptive threshold

The violinists in Ericsson's landmark study had practiced ~10,000 hours by age 20 — but so had second-tier students. The distinction was practice structure, not volume.

## The Comfortable Practice Trap

Experienced practitioners often practice what they're already good at. This produces **fluency** — the feeling of competence — without actual improvement. Deliberate practice targets weak areas specifically, which feels worse but produces gains.

## The Domain Structure Problem

Deliberate practice requires a domain with clear performance criteria and feedback mechanisms. Chess has an objective outcome; music has teachers who can hear technique. Domains with ambiguous criteria (leadership, research, software architecture) are harder to structure for deliberate practice — not because expertise doesn't exist but because the feedback mechanism is unclear.
