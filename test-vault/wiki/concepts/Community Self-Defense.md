# Community Self-Defense

Organized efforts within communities facing targeted violence to keep members safe — historically a feature of labor, anti-fascist, and civil rights movements. Distinct from individualist self-defense: it is collective, political, and oriented toward an alternative to policing.

## Historical Lineage

| Era | Organization | Threat | Response |
|-----|-------------|--------|----------|
| 1905 | Jewish Labour Bund self-defense squads | Pogroms | Armed defense groups in 42 cities |
| 1917 | IWW General Defense Committee | Far-right/KKK attacks on labor | Auxiliary supporting activists facing reprisals |
| 1966 | Black Panther Party | Police violence | Armed patrols, cop watch, legal support |
| 1973 | Lavender Panthers / Purple Panthers | Anti-LGBTQ attacks | Armed defense for San Francisco queer community |
| 2018+ | Socialist Rifle Association, Yellow Peril Tactical, IWW GDC revival | Far-right street violence | Training, mutual aid, digital hygiene |

## Breadth of the Concept

On the left, "self-defense" is far broader than firearms:

- **De-escalation** — trained interveners who lower emotional temperature at protests
- **Street medics** — on-site care for chemical exposure, injuries
- **Digital hygiene** — removing personal data from the internet, secure comms (Yellow Peril Tactical calls this *more important* than guns)
- **Cop watch** — monitoring and documenting police behavior
- **Mutual aid** — food, housing, pandemic response as frontline safety
- **Transformative justice** — accountability processes that address harm without carceral systems
- **Armed training** — firearm skills as one tool among many

## Key Principle: Community-Led

Self-defense only works if it matches the community's expressed desires. The first question is always: "What does safety mean to *you*?" Imposing safety from outside replicates the logic of policing.

"If you want to help a community, they have to trust you." — Snow, Yellow Peril Tactical

## Relationship to Security Culture

[[Security Culture]] provides the operational security framework for *how* these organizations communicate and compartmentalize. Community self-defense is the *what* — the organized capability. Neither works without the other.
