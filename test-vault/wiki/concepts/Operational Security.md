# Operational Security

Operational security (OPSEC) is the set of concrete practices that protect sensitive information and activities from adversaries. Originally a U.S. military concept, it has been adopted across activist, corporate, and personal security contexts.

In activist and organizing contexts, OPSEC is the practical implementation layer of [[Security Culture]] — the specific behaviors that make a security culture concrete rather than aspirational.

## Core Practices

**Information compartmentalization** — the central principle: people should never be privy to sensitive information they do not need to know. Each person holds only the information their role requires. This limits damage from any single compromise or interrogation.

**Communication discipline:**
- Assume unsecured channels are monitored (phone, email, standard SMS)
- Use encrypted communication for sensitive topics (see [[Digital Surveillance Resistance]])
- Develop private shorthand with trusted collaborators for in-person settings

**Physical trail minimization:**
- Credit cards, gas cards, and cell phones leave location and purchase records
- Limit predictability in meeting locations, targets, and timing
- Post scouts; lock doors at meetings; no private residences for sensitive discussions

**Document discipline:**
- Destroy documents when no longer needed; track all written materials
- Written impressions persist on surfaces below; be aware of pad artifacts
- Assume computer use leaves traces

**Social trust:**
- Only collaborate on sensitive projects with people whose history can be verified
- "Vouched for" protocols for unfamiliar attendees at sensitive meetings
- Trust gut reactions; never let social pressure override security instincts

## The Key Inversion

Good OPSEC makes *informant status irrelevant*. CrimethInc. (2004):
> "if your security measures are effective, it shouldn't even matter" whether someone in your network is an infiltrator.

The goal is not to detect adversaries but to make adversary access harmless — because no individual holds enough sensitive information to cause significant damage.
