# Autonomy

In Self-Determination Theory, autonomy is the experience of self-authored behavior — acting from personal values and interests rather than external pressure. It is a basic psychological need whose satisfaction supports intrinsic motivation, learning, and well-being.

## Autonomy ≠ Independence

Autonomy does not require independence. A learner following a structured curriculum can feel autonomous if they've **internalized** the value of the curriculum's goals. Conversely, a fully unconstrained learner can feel controlled if they're acting from fear of failure rather than genuine interest.

The locus of causality distinction: **internal** (I do this because I find it valuable) vs **external** (I do this because I have to).

## What Undermines Autonomy

- Controlling language: *you must*, *you should*, *you have to*
- Rewards contingent on performance (shifts locus of causality external)
- Surveillance and deadlines perceived as controlling
- Evaluation language that emphasizes external judgment over internal progress

These undermine autonomy even when the activity is intrinsically interesting — the **over-justification effect**.

## Autonomy Support

Instructional practices that support autonomy:
- Providing rationale for tasks ("here's why this matters")
- Acknowledging learner perspectives and choices
- Minimizing pressure and evaluation as primary motivators
- Offering meaningful choice within a structure

Autonomy support does not mean removing structure. Structured choice — selecting among constrained options — supports autonomy if the choices are meaningful.

## Connections

- [[Intrinsic Motivation]] — autonomy is one of the three basic needs that support intrinsic motivation; without perceived autonomy, intrinsic motivation doesn't emerge
- [[Self-Regulated Learning]] — self-regulation is an expression of autonomy: the learner sets their own goals, monitors their own progress, and adjusts their own strategy
- [[Scaffolding]] — scaffolding that fades toward independence supports the autonomy need; scaffolding that persists indefinitely undermines it by preventing the internalization of control
