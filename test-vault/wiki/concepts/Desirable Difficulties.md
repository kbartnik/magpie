# Desirable Difficulties

Conditions that make learning feel harder in the short term but produce more durable, transferable learning in the long term.

## The Core Tension

Learning strategies that *feel* most effective (re-reading, massed practice, blocked practice) are the least effective for durable retention. Strategies that *feel* hardest (retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving) are the most effective.

The feeling of productivity and the reality of learning are dissociated.

## Canonical Examples

| Strategy | Why It's Desirable |
|---|---|
| Retrieval practice | Reconstruction strengthens the memory trace |
| Spaced repetition | Partial forgetting forces harder retrieval |
| Interleaving | Discrimination learning transfers to real problems |
| Generation effect | Generating an answer before seeing it produces better encoding than reading it |
| Varying conditions | Learning in multiple contexts improves transfer |

## Undesirable Difficulties

Not all difficulties are desirable. Difficulties are undesirable when they don't produce learning — confusing instructions, illegible text, poorly sequenced content. These increase cognitive load without engaging the learning mechanisms.

The distinction: does the difficulty engage the learning mechanisms (retrieval, discrimination, reconstruction), or does it just make the task harder for unrelated reasons?
