# Visibility Without Pressure Failure Mode

*What determines whether making a problem visible through direct action converts to institutional change, or produces only one news cycle before returning to status quo?*

Shopov's civic hacking playbook (pothole painting) demonstrates that rendering a problem visible through creative direct action can force institutional response. But the analysis focuses on success cases. The failure mode is clear in retrospect: visibility without sustained pressure produces one news cycle and then returns to status quo. The institution absorbs the attention event and resumes normal operation.

What determines the conversion rate from visibility to change? The analysis suggests "material impact" is necessary — the action must create concrete consequences for the institution. But what constitutes material impact varies by institution type: elected officials respond to votes; corporations respond to revenue; administrative agencies respond to legal exposure; police respond to (some) political pressure. The structural conditions for conversion aren't specified.

The deeper question: some problems are structurally resistant to visibility tactics — either because they can't be made visually compelling, or because the institution responsible has sufficient insulation from public pressure. What characterizes the resistant cases?
