# ADHD Disclosure Risk and Policy

*How should workplace accommodation systems be redesigned so that ADHD-diagnosed developers can receive accommodations without facing the disclosure risk that currently deters them?*

GetMeInTheGroove (ICSE 2025) found that 10.6% of programmers have ADHD, with 1.8–4.4× challenge rates on design and time management tasks. Critically: disclosure risk is a primary reason ADHD-diagnosed developers don't request accommodations — they fear professional consequences, stigma, and differential treatment. The accommodation system only works for people who feel safe disclosing, but those who most need accommodations may feel least safe.

Potential policy interventions: anonymous accommodation request processes (request reviewed by HR without manager knowledge until approved); normalizing accommodation in team culture through team-level defaults (async-first communication, no-meeting blocks) that benefit everyone; stronger legal protections against retaliation with accessible enforcement. None of these has been studied specifically in tech workplace contexts.

The parallel question from the monitoring page: if employers can infer ADHD status from productivity monitoring, the disclosure choice may effectively be made for employees — which changes the governance question but may not change the stigma dynamics.
