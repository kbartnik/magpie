# Stoic Acceptance as Rationalization

*Does applying Stoic 'dichotomy of control' to AI disruption risk rationalizing passivity toward harmful AI deployment rather than encouraging principled response?*

Ryan Holiday applies the Stoic dichotomy of control to AI disruption: focus on what you can control (your response, adaptability, skill development) rather than resisting AI adoption broadly. The obstacle-is-the-way reframing treats AI-driven job displacement as an opportunity for growth.

The criticism writes itself: Stoic acceptance of "what is not up to us" can function as ideological cover for not opposing harmful outcomes. If AI automation displaces workers, Stoic advice to adapt individually may serve the interests of those deploying AI by discouraging collective resistance. Identity-decoupling (don't define yourself by your job) may apply differently to knowledge workers who can transition versus manual workers who cannot.

The question is whether Holiday's application is a genuine extension of Stoic philosophy or a convenient misapplication that strips the political context. The Stoics operated under imperial rule where collective political action was genuinely unavailable; the modern context is different. "What is not up to us" is partly determined by whether collective action is available — and collective action to shape AI deployment is available in ways that were not for Epictetus.
