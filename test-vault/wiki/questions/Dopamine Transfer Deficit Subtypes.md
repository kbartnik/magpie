# Dopamine Transfer Deficit Subtypes

*Does the dopamine transfer deficit model apply uniformly across ADHD subtypes, or does inattentive ADHD involve distinct neurobiological mechanisms?*

Tripp & Wickens (2009) propose the dopamine transfer deficit (DTD) model: impaired transfer of dopamine signals to reward-predicting stimuli, explaining why ADHD individuals respond differently to reward contingencies and delayed gratification. The model was developed primarily with evidence from hyperactive/impulsive presentations.

ADHD inattentive subtype presents with different symptoms — attentional drift, working memory impairment, low activation without hyperactivity — that may not map cleanly onto the DTD model. Alternative candidates: default mode network dysregulation (DMN fails to deactivate during tasks), norepinephrine dysregulation (distinct from dopamine), or frontostriatal connectivity differences. If inattentive ADHD has a different primary mechanism, it has different treatment implications — stimulants that primarily affect dopamine may be less effective or require different dosing.

The DSM-5 treats hyperactive-impulsive, inattentive, and combined as presentations of one condition. Whether this reflects a single underlying mechanism across presentations or is a diagnostic convenience is an ongoing debate in the literature.
